The Purity Trap

On how the left forgot what its own ideas were for

March 28, 2026 · ~1,800 words · 12 min read · Unaffiliated
· I ·

An outsider enters a primary. She's 38, raised money on small donations, and within six weeks of announcing has become what campaigns spend millions to manufacture: a genuine moment. She speaks directly to people without a teleprompter. She answers questions she wasn't asked. She touches on the years she spent figuring things out in a way that feels true, and people listen and see themselves in her. The crowds are atypically young and disaffiliated. She is, by any reasonable measure, exactly what her party says it wants on the national stage.

Then an unravelling occurs.

It doesn't announce itself as opposition. It surfaces first as a question — maybe a local reporter asking for comment on something from years ago. A politico-type with a niche and hyper-engaged following tweets it. Then a quote from a "longtime Democratic strategist" expressing "concern." The institutions that shape these races — campaign committees, donor networks, legacy endorsers — haven't officially weighed in. They won't need to. Their work happens at a remove: supplying coverage, seeding doubts, maintaining plausible distance while the narrative churns into itself. This is a system, and it is ours. By the time it's completed, the candidate people had been looking for is no longer a person but a collection of anecdotes, receipts, and liabilities that have been found.

Ethan Agarwal is one example. A 40-year-old tech founder challenging Ro Khanna in California's 17th congressional district, he is a Democrat — pro-choice, pro-gun control, pro-immigration. His record as a founder includes a music licensing dispute with Universal Music Group resulting in a $2 million confession of judgment, a subsequent failure to pay $300,000 of the agreed settlement, and separate litigation over unpaid commercial rent. He downloaded pornography. He went through a divorce. These facts have been assembled and presented to voters as a character verdict. Whether they constitute one is the question this essay is asking — while final judgment, for now, belongs where it should: to the voters of California's 17th.

· II ·

The establishment's most compelling case against politically inexperienced, flawed candidates was made not by a party operative but by Immanuel Kant. The 18th-century moral philosopher whose framework still haunts what we now call accountability culture was precise about this: a wrong act is a wrong act. No life circumstance excuses moral responsibility. No candidate's identification with nonconformity justifies their shortcomings. The excavation of a candidate's past is not unjust persecution — it is due diligence, and it is the political establishment's own form of natural selection. For the parties, it's the primal work of discerning whether someone seeking public trust can win a general electorate and, once elected, act from civic duty rather than personal impulse. To ask what someone has done is to ask who they are. It is not an unreasonable question to ask of someone who wants to speak and hold power on your behalf.

Kant's logic is not incorrect. Vetting is not bad, records are not irrelevant, and in the pursuit of fresh faces, not everything goes. Some candidates are genuinely unfit, and these mechanisms have kept plenty of bad actors from becoming elected officials. That much is true and worth saying plainly.

But there is a page the operators of these mechanisms have not turned.

On Moral Agency — Immanuel Kant

"Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."

— Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, 1785

Kant's entire moral architecture — the categorical imperative, the universalizability of ethical law, the dignity of rational persons — rests on a foundational assumption: that rational agents can recognize a wrong act, take responsibility for it, and reorient their will toward the good. This is not a footnote. It is the load-bearing premise. A political culture that treats past wrongdoing as a permanent, defining verdict does not extend Kantian accountability — it dismantles it. It removes the only faculty that makes accountability meaningful: the capacity to reckon and reform. You cannot believe in moral responsibility and deny the possibility of moral recovery. The two are not in tension. They are incompatible.

What purity culture has constructed, then, is not a more rigorous ethics. It is a more convenient one. It demands nothing of the political community — no judgment, no discernment, no tolerance for complexity — and everything of the candidate. The verdict is rendered before the argument is heard.

· III ·

The American left built its moral framework on a serious idea: that human beings are not naturally good or bad but are shaped by systems, incentives, and circumstances — and that the right response to human failure is accountability, rehabilitation, and structural redesign. This is the root of the left's language of inclusion. Its skepticism of carceral logic. Its belief in second chances. Its insistence that people be judged in context rather than in the abstract.

These are not personality traits that liberals happen to have. They are ideals, consciously constructed in opposition to a world that defaults to something harsher. They exist precisely because their architects understood that people act out of alignment with their values. That's not an unfortunate exception to human nature — it is human nature. The whole project was built on that premise. Which makes it worth asking what, exactly, the project is doing when it decides a licensing dispute or a downloaded file is a character verdict.

Purity culture isn't really about standards. It's about control. It's about who gets to decide who's acceptable and on what grounds — a power mechanism that wears the costume of ethics.

The figure that purity culture has been quietly producing is what I'd call the political god: the elected official who has fully traded legislating and governing for performing the superiority of their ideology. Propped up by national profile and institutional loyalty, these are politicians whose biographies are clean because they were curated to be clean. Michel Foucault described this dynamic in institutional contexts — the way surveillance systems cause people to police themselves so thoroughly that the institution barely needs to act. The candidate who has spent a career pre-laundering their biography has done exactly this: internalized the gaze of judgment so completely that nothing survives that could be found. The system didn't disqualify them. They disqualified themselves, preemptively, from everything human in their record.

The result is not virtue. It is the simulation of virtue. And we have been electing the simulation long enough to see where it leads — a governing class performing ideological purity for a national audience while the actual work of governance quietly goes undone.

· IV ·

Graham Platner is running for Senate in Maine. Ethan Agarwal is running in California. They are young, they have records, and something will be found. That is nearly certain. What is less certain — and far more important — is what we do when it is.

We are in the first cycle where candidates native to the internet are running in meaningful numbers against a party infrastructure that is not. Democratic primary voters skew older, more educated, and whiter than the general electorate — a group that learned to process information before social media restructured what information looks like. When a single decontextualized sentence moves through that ecosystem, it functions as a verdict, not an argument. He's a fraud. She's compromised. It requires no further reading. The candidate then spends the remainder of the race arguing against a sentence, which is the worst position in politics: reactive, explanatory, defensive. And explanation, in this media environment, is indistinguishable from guilt.

Six House Democrats died in office in the last thirteen months. All in their seventies. The party is, in a literal sense, waiting for its membership to expire before considering renewal. And while it waits, it is cultivating an electorate trained to mistake the absence of a visible record for the presence of character.

· V ·

The sanitized candidate — the one who arrives in politics with nothing findable — is not produced by high standards. They are produced by a process that selects for risk aversion above all else. It's worth being honest about who that process favors. The candidate without a complicated financial history, without a messy decade, without the documented failures that accumulate when you've been trying things and losing at some of them — that candidate has almost always had access to the conditions that prevent those records from forming in the first place. Wealth insulates. Education provides the language to manage exposure. Family absorbs the failures that, in other lives, become liens and lawsuits. The purity standard does not apply equally. It never has. It simply appears neutral because the people who designed it were already protected by it.

That's not a new generation. That's the same generation, managed better.

On Natality & Political Beginnings — Hannah Arendt

"The miracle that saves the world, the realm of human affairs, from its normal, 'natural' ruin is ultimately the fact of natality... the birth of new men and the new beginning, the action they are capable of by virtue of being born."

— The Human Condition, 1958

Hannah Arendt argued that natality — the human capacity to begin something genuinely new — is not merely a metaphor for political hope but the central category of political thought itself. Not legacy, not continuity: the irreducible capacity for new beginnings, rooted in the uniqueness of each person who enters public life. A system that closes off those beginnings by treating the past as permanent definition is not protecting political life. It is ending it, slowly, from the inside.

Campaigns that want to win must address their candidates' flaws directly and honestly — that burden is real. But the deeper malpractice does not belong to the individual. It belongs to a collective that has confused the absence of a visible record for the presence of character, and in doing so has disqualified the only thing that might actually change something: a representative who has made mistakes, knows it, and has chosen — with full awareness of the cost — to be accountable for them anyway.

The political gods we've been electing have never had to make that choice.

It shows.
